The Tools and Tips of Immersive Learning

 

Introduction

         Spending time in a foreign country greatly benefits anyone who wants to learn a new language.  In-country immersion, where a person becomes part of a local community and interacts daily with native language speakers, offers numerous opportunities for a person to take advantage of learning the language.  With immersion, learning does not have to be costly, and although it does not require massive amounts of time, a person will find the more they put into it, the more they will get out of it.

 

What Are Some Freebies of Immersion?

         How much of a language can you get for free just by living in a native speaking country? How much can you learn of a language without taking lessons? Here are three freebies I have discovered in my travels that can greatly aid a foreigner and are easily obtained:

1. Constant exposure enables you to pick up basic words. When you join a new community that speaks a foreign language, you repeatedly encounter certain basic words that are almost a necessity to get by. These would include words such as “hello”, “goodbye”, “please”, “thank you”, “I’m sorry”, and “excuse me”.  A wise traveler should seek to learn words like these upon arrival.  When staying in a foreign country, very little effort is actually required to learn basic words of the local language since you tend to assimilate them merely because it is too difficult to navigate daily life without knowing them.

2. Daily life provides opportunities to learn the language. A second way to obtain local language skills comes from ordering things such as food, services, and merchandise. In daily life, even if someone you tried to avoid interacting with people as much as possible, there is one thing you cannot avoid: ordering things!   Getting groceries, eating at a restaurant, and shopping for clothes are all situations where you cannot avoid interacting.  This repetition teaches even the most indifferent learner to inevitably pick up whatever words or expressions are required to ensure the simplest, smoothest interaction.

3. You learn words for essential items as a part of daily living.  Since you repeatedly come across the words for items such as food, drinks, groceries, or clothes, when you order these things required for everyday life, you have repeated opportunities and practice to learn more of a language.  The repetition of seeing the words for these items so many times when you live in a new country instills the words in your brain naturally. I estimate this acquisition of words to take usually 2-8 weeks depending on how actively a person seeks to learn.

 

How Little Can You Learn?

         People attribute immersion with being the golden secret to learning a language. They make it sound as if all you have to do is spend time in a particular country, and then picking up the language will just happen. Although, there is a huge advantage to spending time in the country, personally, I do not believe this alone is enough.

Let us consider just how little you can possibly learn by putting in very little effort, whether intentional or not. Aside from the three “freebies” I listed, I believe the answer is none! You can live in a country where you do not know the language and essentially acquire none of it! This was evidenced by my roommate in central Europe, as well as some of my friends in other countries who have been abroad for both a semester and for a decade. None of them learned very much of the local language during their time of stay.

If you are not passionate about a language and do things the wrong way, immersing yourself in a language does not automatically result in learning. Lydia Machova’s presentation at the 2016 Gathering demonstrates effectively the power that being interested or disinterested in a language has when it comes to acquiring a language. Teachers greatly struggle to educate students in a language they do not want to learn. In addition, when a traveler or foreigner does not even have a teacher to guide or compel them, they may become disinclined or unwilling to learn the new language. For this reason, many people can live in a new country for years and still only be able to speak very basic parts of the native language. Thus, immersion is by no means a magic tool that automatically teaches a person something; however, it does have several tremendous advantages.

 

 

 

Why Is Immersion So Effective?

It does not take a lot of effort for immersion to be effective. Many people have had a lot of their language acquisition success through the privilege of spending time immersed in the country that speaks the native language.

Here are some of the reasons immersion is so effective:

1.  It provides immediate examples. When you learn something, maybe a word or grammatical case or set of endings, you immediately see it in use when you go into the community. Things you just learned are reinforced, and new things are constantly being taught. I remember when I was in Poland the first time, in the beginning, there were so many sets of endings of which I had no idea of the meaning. For example, I would see numerous words sometimes ending in –ami – ów – ą –ach, which in a Slavic language represents different cases.  So, each week when I would learn a new case and its endings, it was like receiving a piece of a puzzle and everywhere I looked I would immediately see examples of what I had just learned. Because of this constant exposure, I never needed to spend extra time studying or reminding myself of these cases or words because the repetition and examples I would see outside reaffirmed what I had learned and drilled it into my brain.

2.  There is no lack of practice partners! Everywhere you go provides an opportunity to speak the native language. Opportunities to put your knowledge to practice abound, sometimes whether you want to or not.

3.  Local friends are available who expose you to new situations. Nothing opens up a new world quite like local friends do. Making friends who know the language and city gives you a chance to settle in and live life like a genuine native speaker of the languages does. Making local friends also provides a much-appreciated sense of comfort, variety, and fun, often when it is most needed. Opportunities to make friends of this nature usually only come from immersion into the local community.

4.  You come across situations where people do not speak English. Since a significant number of citizens in foreign countries speak English proficiently, many visitors, unfortunately, do not consider proper language etiquette and thus, take advantage of English speakers to accommodate themselves. Additionally, you often may get tired of putting forth the mental effort required to endure new situations in a foreign language, so it is just easier to give into the temptation to speak only in English or your native language. Both of these things are especially true with beginners. Regardless of the country, though, people still exist that do not speak any English. Situations like these will force you to rely completely on your new language. In Poland, I needed to buy headache medicine and anti-itch cream. I went in with a few specific words pre-prepared to communicate. However, I had to communicate to the pharmacist many things I did not expect and had not prepared. Since she did not speak any English, she had to teach me a lot of words in any way she could. After this experience, I never forgot the Polish words for the parts of the body.

5.  Immersive living advances fluency.  Although “fluency” is one of those terms defined a thousand different ways, I think, in truth, it is a word that describes how smoothly you can utilize a language to meet your needs. The key being for whatever is needed. The best way to identify what you need to learn to reach a high level of fluency for your own life is to actually live life and thus discover what is essential by experience. Part of why immersion is the quickest way to fluency is because it is the shortest path.

6.  Immersion provides self-regulation.  Related to the previous point, the beauty of immersive learning is the instant feedback provided to you, as well as the ability to tailor learning to your personal needs. If you go to a barbershop and need to tell the barber what length you want the different parts of your hair to be, you discover quickly the need to learn the words for lengths, as well as the words for sides and top and anything else that pertains to getting a haircut. If you learn the names of different foods and drinks, you can go to a restaurant and immediately affirm what you have learned. The instant use and validation of what is learned and what is necessary as determined by your personal life makes the progression in which you learn highly specific to the individual and highly relevant to your personal needs. This proves extremely helpful for retention due to its usefulness, personal application, and accessibility.

 

 

How Can You Gain the Most Benefit?  

What can you do to optimize the way you learn a new language in an immersive environment? Here are seven tips and tricks you can use to get the most out of any opportunity.

1.  Find a native-speaking teacher. A person may question why it is any more important to have a teacher in the foreign country than it is in your home country.  I admit that some reasonable doubt exists as to whether it is necessary. Thus, I would not recommend spending too much time or money with a teacher, but having one provides a lot of value because it provides you with an expert who thoroughly knows the native language and can monitor your learning. Every language has a different path of progression. There are questions that a teacher can clarify:  When should you use these tenses? These conjugations? These parts of speech? A teacher also cares about optimizing your learning in order to guide and lead you down the smoothest path for easily identifying what parts of the language to focus on next.

2.  Take advantage of available language exchanges.  Language exchanges are great. For one thing, they give you an opportunity to make friends with native speakers.  This provides the added mental and emotional benefits of helping your mind settle in to the new community and feel more at home. Secondly, meeting a native speaker for language exchange gives you a chance to learn anything you want and to simulate any situation you want or think is necessary to know. It is a great free way to get useful help in any area.

3.  Utilize Google. For my part, as a software engineer interested in artificial intelligence to create an accurate translation tool, I am not a huge fan of Google’s approach to translation and look with excitement for much better tools available in the future.  However, for the purposes discussed in this paper, Google is a brilliant tool that can be used to fill in gaps in vocabulary that are needed in everyday life. I personally think using Google to speak a language has a small stigma attached to it because it does not honestly portray a person’s knowledge of a language at a given time, and instead allows you to cheat and appear to know more than you actually do. However, this is admittedly silly because we are not trying to prove how much of a language you can speak flawlessly at any given time. The ultimate goal is to learn a language, and practicing it in real life situations is the best way to accomplish this goal. For this reason, Google is great because, for example, if I am on my way to a new gym, I can use Google Translate to find words like “membership” or “single-entrance” and can then use them instantly and commit those words to long-term memory through instant practice. Or, if I am in public and need to ask someone a question and don’t know how, I can instantly get the answer from Google and apply it. During my time abroad, I often tried to find new things I could say or ask people and would Google the words and then apply them. For me, this was probably the most effective way to learn and remember new phrases of a language.

4.  Keep a list of important words. Some words seem to appear repeatedly, and it becomes clear they are important words; however, they may be difficult to remember.  Therefore, my fourth tip is to make a list of important words. I came across many of these important but easily forgotten words, so I began to save a list of them on my phone, which turned into a small dictionary of words that were highly relevant to me. I could refer to my list at any time and get quick answers. I was constantly adding new words and removing old words that I had eventually used so much they became unforgettable.

5.   Use abundantly available translators. For some reason, some people pressure themselves to learn a foreign language all on their own, as if they want to prove they can do it. This is a flawed expectation from a learning perspective. A wonderful advantage of immersive learning is that there are translators everywhere. When in a store, a gym, or a bus, you should not be afraid to ask someone how to say a word or phrase of interest because there are numerous benefits to making the effort to inquire. For one, you can trust the correctness of what you are taught because a local native speaker is guaranteed to give a natural, context-appropriate translation, which is something Google can never provide.  Second, in my experience I have never had a negative response to my asking for the translation or pronunciation of a word. This has an added benefit towards helping a visitor feel included and comfortable in a new country. Locals in most countries love to see foreigners take an interest in their language and try to learn it. Asking a stranger how to say a word or phrase creates a great opportunity to have a healthy and friendly interaction with the local community.

6.  Try new words.  Say something new for the sake of saying something new. This is a great way to learn. It is natural for you to want to stick to talking about things you are confident in, but to learn new words and optimize the time in a foreign country, a person must try out new words and phrases. One of my greatest successes occurred whenever I was on a bus and tried to figure out how to say where I was going and then tell someone so I could use the new word.  I am a very quiet person who does not usually speak unless necessary, but in a foreign language I liked to challenge myself and say whatever words I could whenever possible. I believe this benefited me greatly in how efficiently I was able to learn.

7.  Visit local places where people do not speak much English. In most countries, there are groups that are more likely to speak English, typically younger people, and groups that are less likely, usually the older generation. A person who wants to learn a new language should go somewhere people are not as likely to speak English and interact with them. Even better is not to tell anyone that you speak English. Many locals have a misunderstanding of language etiquette and think being polite means to speak English instead of the local language. However, people do not hesitate if you pretend to be clueless when English is spoken and act as if you only understand words spoken in the native language. Richard Simcott, a hyperpolyglot who lives in the UK said the following about language etiquette:

“If somebody comes to my country to speak my language, I give them the general courtesy to speak my language to them. Because they’ve invested time, they’ve invested money, they’ve invested resources to come and learn my language. If I go to your country to learn your language, you should speak to me in your language. Even if I absolutely suck at the language. You should speak to me in it so that I improve, because I am investing time and money.”

 

How Can You Manage Fatigue?

Lastly, the issue of managing mental fatigue exists. It is mentally challenging to constantly work on learning a language without any relief. Being in a foreign country means you can never stop practicing and learning. Therefore, it is very important to ensure your mind rests and stays encouraged.

There are a few ways to ensure you remain encouraged.  One of them is to track progress. As time goes on, it starts to feel like maybe progress has stalled and possibly stopped altogether.  This is rarely true. A good way to determine progress is to keep markers, which can provide a statistical measure of progress made. I found a helpful way to measure my personal progress was by my weekly visits to church.  The first week I understood nothing, but each week I progressively understood more than I did the previous week. Despite believing that I was learning very little of the language, I realized with each church visit that it was a little bit easier for me to understand the preacher than it was the time before. Another helpful way to measure progress is to ask friends to provide feedback. It is also encouraging to take time to think back on how much of a struggle there was with some words that no longer pose difficulty. Find a consistent activity in life and compare weekly progress.

A second way to stay positive is to set reasonable goals and then remind yourself that you are meeting or exceeding expectations, not falling short of them. One of the things about learning a new language is that you learn a lot very quickly. When you learn in an environment immersed in the native language, and do it the way this essay recommends, you truly learn a lot very quickly. During the first few months, it is easy and natural not to expect significant results since it is a new language and you are not supposed to know it. When little is expected and then you learn something, you feel you are exceeding expectations. However, after a few months you start to feel like you should be speaking at a high level, with some amount of fluency by this point. You can begin to put pressure on yourself and get frustrated when making mistakes you considered acceptable in the beginning. It is fine to expect a lot from yourself, but not accepting inevitable mistakes will exhaust and discourage you. Again, you should use progress tracking or friends to remember that you are still exceeding expectations, even if the expectations have increased.

Also, remember that mistakes are valuable. They are how you learn and are ways to identify weak areas where you need improvement. Consider mistakes to be opportunities that allow you to keep improving and do not think they mean progress has stalled. Therefore, you need to constantly remind yourself that you are continuing to learn at a high rate, and just because your expectations increase does not mean your progress is not increasing too.

Lastly, find a favorite, familiar spot where you feel confident and comfortable. It helps tremendously to find a place to go anytime you need to relax where you can complete the verbal exchange with ease. I found coffee shops to be this type of place for me.  Since I ordered coffee more times than anything else, I felt the most self-assured in this situation. Sometimes I became discouraged because I would make a mistake somewhere or could not understand someone when I thought I should.  I would take that frustration and go order a coffee, where I already knew they would ask me about size and type of coffee, with or without milk, whether it was to go, and if there was anything else I wanted.   I knew exactly how to respond to all of these questions in the most fluent manner. The success of the exchange would remind me how much I love foreign languages and how much I had learned. Joy would replace my stress and frustration. Consequently, I recommend for everyone to find a situation that is easily repeatable and consistently successful so it is possible to find that same kind of joy and sense of accomplishment when speaking a new language.
